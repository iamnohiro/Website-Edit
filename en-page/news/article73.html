Brazil Launched New Telecom Monitoring

Brazil's Telecommunications Agency, Anatel recently launched a telecommunications monitoring system in the capital. This was reported by federal news agency Agência Brasil on Monday.

Although the total investment amount for the system was not disclosed to the public, the system will enable Anatel to optimize the quality of service, increase overall work efficiency and prevent disruptions in telecom services. This will also help in monitoring the performance of physical infrastructure but will not assess communication content or data, according to the news agency.

The said technology has already been tested during the month-long FIFA World Cup which was held in Rio de Janeiro in Brazil. Anatel also expressed plans to expand this monitoring service to pay TV, fixed telephony services as well as fixed broadband services by the end of the first half of 2015.

This initiative may be in line with the establishment of Brazil's Management Board for the Development of M2M systems. Both initiatives aim to gather regulate the activities involved the development and enhancement of services, infrastructure and the emergence of new trends for these telecom sectors. The board will likewise administer to suppliers of telecom administrations, makers, administrators, and examination & advancement organizations, to further improve and develop the nation's M2m industry.

Furthermore, a new minimum fixed speed for mobile broadband has been established by the agency and will be enforced on the first of November. Telecom operators need to guarantee that the end-users and consumers get an average of at least 80 percent of the speed as opposed to only 70 percent last year. They are required to deliver at least 40 percent of the contracted speed at any given moment, which is an increase of 10 percent to the speed delivered last year.

Meantime, fixed broadband communications as well as personal mobile communication services are also regulated, which took effect in 2012. This is still in response to the government's new speed requirements.
